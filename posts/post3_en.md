Indeed, I am a Ye enthusiast.

### Background Introduction

I had virtually zero exposure to hip-hop music prior to this; as a junior listener, I offer my apologies to the veterans here. After encountering the artist's "My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy" in July, I became irrevocably captivated; the more I listened, the more substance I found, hence the composition of this article as a memorial. Furthermore, the author previously gravitated primarily toward rock music; adhering to the philosophy that "a brilliant melody transcends its lyrics," the analytical weight of the lyrics here will be marginally subordinate to the arrangement. Now let us commence the formal rating, with the album sequence determined solely by chronological release.

---

### 1. The College Dropout
**Rating: "S"**

As Ye's debut album, "The College Dropout" exhibited his formidable production prowess established during his tenure as a behind-the-scenes producer...

I jest; let us proceed directly to our subjective impression segment. Every instance I hear the artist's accent in the intro, it elicits a laugh. While relishing his superb articulation and still reminiscing, the listener is abruptly interrupted, plunged into the exhilarating rhythm of "We Don't Care." After savoring Ye's unique, high-pitched vocal delivery, the track seamlessly transitions into a children's chorus. This stark contrast is inherently amusing. Simultaneously, the lyrics inject the ethos of "life is short, make merry while you can" into the album's genetic makeup.

The album possesses robust narrative continuity, with virtually no jarring transitions between singles, making it ideal for a comprehensive, uninterrupted listening session.
* **Track Recommendations**: Ensure you listen to "Through the Wire" and "Family Business."

### 2. Late Registration
**Rating: "A+"**

The commencement of this sophomore effort retains the artist's bizarre, comical, and hearty laughter; each listen compels me to ponder the anatomical origins of such an accent. Following a soothing musical prelude, the listener is met with the familiar, sudden offensive of the petulant Ye.

It remains extremely cohesive, but in juxtaposition with "The College Dropout," the overarching atmosphere is markedly more serious, extending from the cover art to the musical arrangement. Moreover, the sophistication of the sampling has palpably advanced compared to its predecessor.

### 3. Graduation
**Rating: "S+"**

The most euphoric album; everyone should experience "Stronger." Upon my initial encounter with this single, I experienced a visceral numbness. Unquestionably, the sole individual capable of sampling Daft Punk to such an exceptionally exalted standard is West himself.

"Graduation," operating as a pop-centric album, heralded the old Kanye's transition from Old School rap to an intersecting fusion of pop, psychedelic, electronic music, and hip-hop. If you are exploring rap for the first time, this album serves as an optimal entry point; the entire project exudes a profound pop sensibility, highly suitable for listeners accustomed to mainstream pop. Concurrently, the rhythmic intensity is profound, establishing it as a mandatory soundtrack for my exercise routines.
* **Track Recommendations**: Remember to listen to the single "I Wonder"; it is also exceptional.

### 4. 808s & Heartbreak
**Rating: "A"**

This album belongs to the category of works that reveal their depth through repeated consumption. When I first listened to "Say You Will," the background rhythm, simulating a heartbeat, induced a profound sense of oppression. After a few attempts failing to resonate with it, I switched albums. The evaluation for this record was penned retroactively, after I had completed subsequent reviews and returned to it. Upon re-listening, I can only concede it was a misjudgment; Kanye weaponized Auto-Tune and synthesizers to their absolute zenith in this project.

"808s & Heartbreak," arriving as West's inaugural project following the traumas of divorce and the passing of his mother, Donda, somewhat mirrors his psychological trajectory after navigating the abyss of losing his closest kin. Possessing this historical context significantly alters the listener's psychological state. The overarching sensation the album imparts is one of a desperate hope.
* **Track Recommendations**: Try "Welcome to Heartbreak" and "Heartless."

### 5. My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy
**Rating: "S+"**

Indisputably, this stands as the old Kanye's magnum opus. If circumstance permits you to listen to merely one Kanye album, let it be this.
* **Track Recommendations**: Remember to listen to "Runaway."

### 6. Watch The Throne
**Rating: "B"**

Despite being a collaborative effort between West and Jay-Z, there is a distinct sensation that Jay-Z asserts a more dominant presence throughout the record. The entire album leans heavily toward Old School aesthetics, and Ye's signature sampling techniques do not occupy a significant proportion of the sonic landscape. To my ear, the latter half of the album proves more opulent and engaging. I lack an affinity for gangsta rap.
* **Track Recommendations**: Try "Niggas in Paris" and "Made in America."

### 7. Yeezus
**Rating: "B+"**

Ye's foray into the realm of industrial music; those who appreciate it will revere it, while for me, it remains excessively avant-garde and temporarily beyond my comprehension.
* **Track Recommendations**: Try "Bound 2." A love ballad West dedicated to Kim.

### 8. The Life of Pablo
**Rating: "S"**

What remains to be said? The entire album represents a regression to West's comfort zone: vocal choirs and elite-tier sampling permeate "The Life of Pablo." One could argue this album delineates the frontier between the old Kanye and the new Ye. I lament the absence of the old Kanye.
* **Track Recommendations**: Highly recommend "Ultralight Beam," "Father Stretch My Hands, Pt. 1," and "Famous."

### 9. Jesus Is King
**Rating: "S"**

If "The Life of Pablo" constituted West's preliminary foray into gospel music, then "Jesus Is King" embodies the zenith of Ye's gospel achievements. As Ye's premier gospel album, he unreservedly articulates his devotion to God; Ye vanquished his bipolar disorder through faith. The integration of the Sunday Service Choir imbues this album with an undeniable divinity. Were I to awaken tomorrow as a devout Christian, this album's rating would ascend to "S+".
* **Track Recommendations**: Highly recommend "Every Hour," "Selah," and "Closed on Sunday." If intrigued, explore the live collaborative performances between the Sunday Service Choir and Ye.

---

### Conclusion

West operates more akin to an artist than a conventional rapper; his transcendent sampling and arrangement proficiencies are likely pivotal factors in his acquisition of multiple Grammys. I harbor hope that he comprehensively overcomes his bipolar disorder soon, delivering further magnificent albums to us, and I wish for a world eradicated of illness.
