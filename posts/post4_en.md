> In previous texts, I referenced an example regarding "eye contact": when experiencing awkwardness while locking eyes with a stranger, attempt to project yourself into the pedestrian's perspective, and the awkwardness dissolves. This illustrates the nuanced utility of "empathy" (perspective-taking) at the individual level.
> 
> Throughout this recent period, I seem to have acquired a novel inspiration: can this logical framework be transposed from the individual to the collective? When I endeavor to expand the subject to encompass a group, numerous previously perplexing phenomena suddenly attain transparency.

### 01 / Group Substitution and Mirror Mapping

Permit me to construct an example: male readers might harbor bewilderment toward demographics labeled as "K-pop fangirls," or even experience distress regarding so-called "fandom culture"; conversely, female readers occasionally find it arduous to empathize with the fixations of "otaku bros" or specific "gaming circles."

*(Herein, we temporarily appropriate these two highly emblematic subcultural groups as "mental models"—not to fortify stereotypes, but to cultivate a contextual framework for "empathy.")*

However, if we execute the **substitution method**: transposing the subject with a familiar, same-sex demographic (for instance, equating "fandom support" to "football match fanaticism" or "pay-to-win gaming behaviors"), we become capable of comprehending the underlying commonalities beneath the perplexity, rather than anchoring prejudices to specific collectives.

The aforementioned "substitution" can be further evolved into **mirror mapping**: perceiving the oppositional faction as a mirror reflection of our own.

Every internal group dynamic harbors a spectrum ranging from 'moderate' to 'radical.' Upon encountering extremists from the opposing camp, one might immediately query their own consciousness—does our camp not possess equally fanatical, even irrational individuals?

The answer is invariably affirmative. This psychological mapping rapidly facilitates our "demystification" and "desensitization": the adversary's extremist rhetoric does not constitute the entirety of their reality, just as our extremists fail to represent me. Consequently, we can establish a mechanism for **contrapuntal dialogue**: autonomously filtering out individuals rendered communicatively void by extreme radicalism, seeking solely those possessing comparable rationality for discourse, thereby mitigating the exhaustion inherent in talking past one another.

### 02 / The Elephant in the Room and the Anima

Let us pivot our perspective from the collective back to the individual.

I have progressively realized that the emotional, vulnerable "maternal" element residing within me has, in fact, perpetually operated as an "elephant in the room." Though silent, it has commanded an irrefutable seat during all my historical, critical decision-making processes.

On one occasion, I half-jokingly remarked to my mother: "Mom, sometimes I perceive myself as somewhat 'androgynous'." Subsequently, in pursuit of the logic underwriting this sensation, I consulted academic materials, only to discover that Carl Jung had long ago articulated the concepts of the **Anima** and **Animus**.

In that instant, a synchronous sensation of "all scriptures annotating me" induced a visceral reaction: my minor epiphanies had long been deciphered by my predecessors.

I employ this to demonstrate that, occasionally, we must permit our internal, oppositional viewpoints to occupy the same negotiating table, rather than endeavoring to "gag" a specific faction. When we acclimatize to this internal dialectic, eschewing spiritual castration, the self that emerges will possess substantially greater resilience.
