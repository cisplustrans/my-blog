I likely encountered this book in my deskmate’s bookshelf; it was titled *Different Seasons*. I remember thinking: isn't Stephen King supposed to be a writer of horror? Little did I know that on that afternoon, I would spend the most exhilarating two periods of physics class of my life. Three years have passed in a flash, and on another afternoon, after watching *Stand By Me* in my dormitory, I realized that this was indeed that novella, "The Body."

Forgive me for not offering a conventional introduction to the film here. It is a profound piece of cinema; I hope you can experience these 88 minutes and 34 seconds for yourself.

Following my last post, someone asked me: "What is the meaning of university?"

*Stand By Me* and its core concept—"Coming of Age"—seem to provide an answer.

![The Boys](images/post2-01.jpg)
<small><i>Fig 1. The four boys in 'Stand By Me' — a quintessential 'coming-of-age' narrative.</i></small>

What, then, is "Coming of Age"?

In a narrative sense, it refers to the psychological, moral, and identity transition of a protagonist—usually an adolescent—from childhood to adulthood. The focus is not on the external events, but on the internal maturation, the loss of innocence, and the navigation of a "rite of passage" or a pivotal experience to face the world.

I view the current state of confusion among university students as a consequence of a missing "Coming of Age." Collectivism confines our lives within a predetermined trajectory, as if everyone's growth is replaced by a fixed set of protocols: study, exam, work, marriage, reproduction. It seems our growth must be oriented toward realizing social value and providing productivity. This societal atmosphere leaves us no time to pause for self-reflection or exploration.

Recall those moments: when you were drawn to a philosophical question during a compulsory political science class, was there always a classmate complaining, "These classes are useless; can thinking about these questions put food on the table?"

Perhaps this is the same reason for the prohibition of "early romance." The sense of achievement and happiness found in love can be detached from that "evaluation system." One recognizes oneself through "recognizing others," discovering the brilliance in the other, which in turn mirrors one's own.

Our voices of self-exploration are not permitted to be heard.

Yet, one need not be so pessimistic. As economic growth slows and information flows with unprecedented speed, it is natural for individuals to turn inward, seeking self-knowledge and reconciliation. The iceberg of this society is slowly melting.

Reading this, you might expect me to provide an ultimate answer—a definitive guide to self-growth. My response is: I am afraid I cannot.

I could offer some irresponsible answers, but you would likely forget them within three minutes of finishing this article.

University is precisely the best opportunity for you to discover the answers to these questions within the endless struggle and confrontation with yourself.


Next time you find yourself in a "useless class," consider this: some efforts do not require immediate returns. Those unintentional explorations and reflections might just be the most vital nourishment for your growth.
