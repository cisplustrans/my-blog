The first time I opened this book was probably during a Maoism class.

The campus Wi-Fi, which refused to connect no matter what, left me with no choice but to flip through previously downloaded ebooks. I genuinely had no impression of this book, *Demian: The Story of Emil Sinclair's Youth*. I didn't expect that this novel, opened by accident during a Maoism class, would resonate so perfectly with my current state of mind.

To briefly recall the plot: It is about a boy named Sinclair who, guided by his mysterious friend Demian, breaks the "egg" of traditional concepts of good and evil. He goes through spiritual struggles and reshaping, ultimately achieving the awakening of his personality and a return to his true self.

There is a quote in the book I really like:

> "The bird fights its way out of the egg. The egg is the world. Who would be born must first destroy a world."

A long while after finishing this book, I increasingly understood the implications of Hesse's "egg." I want to ask: can this be simply understood as having a similar meaning to Tyler's quote in *Fight Club*, "Self-improvement is masturbation"?

Self-improvement is like masturbation. If we cannot break the eggshell of the old values wrapped around us, isn't everything we do just self-deception inside the shell? Self-improvement within the eggshell is built on social expectations from the very beginning. What does it have to do with our true nature? Or rather, has our nature already been hijacked by the nature of society? Is all of this really what the core self desired when we were first born?

I want to give an example: old values are like the path social rules expect us to take—from high school to college, and then on to grad school or work. Society demands we walk this path, but does anyone reflect on the purpose of it all? In most cases, people choose to blindfold themselves, mistake the process for the result, and blindly follow the path to the dark end.

I am not saying there is anything inherently wrong with this path. In fact, it is the simplest route an ordinary person can take to achieve "success."

I just want everyone to think about this: if this path leads to a dead end, or if something goes wrong at a certain node, how should we choose to face or accept the consequences of failure?

In my view, the process of shaping your own values acts as an insurance policy. It pulls you up when you make a mistake, giving you a chance to cut your losses, instead of making you feel like the sky is falling and driving you to jump off a building.

---

This is exactly why I put the phrase "try to live in accord with the promptings which came from my true self" in the title.

Actually, if we shed the eggshell of social discipline covering us, living according to our nature is nothing but a matter of course.

The mission of human beings, from the moment we are born into this world, is simply to gather energy, survive, reproduce, and die.

Everything else, all other behaviors, are merely meanings assigned by the brain.

Imagine if an apocalyptic disaster struck tomorrow, and only a tiny fraction of life—including yourself—survived. Would you choose to end your life just because you couldn't go to grad school, couldn't work, and couldn't make money? I think most people would choose to survive.

Therefore, barring severe physical trauma (including pathological mental illness), I oppose any act of ending one's life lightly.

---

Please forgive my rambling.

The protagonist Sinclair met a close friend like Demian during his coming-of-age. Unfortunately, Hesse is not God. He didn't bless every ordinary person with an almost angelic friend like that. I can only move forward alone, just as the book says:

> "You must listen within yourself, and then you will notice that I am within you."

To face Kromer, to face the battlefield, to pursue Frau Eva.

I hope everyone can truly live for themselves for once, live according to their spontaneous nature for once.

Of course, perhaps when I look back at what I'm writing now in three or five years, these words might seem like forcing sorrow for the sake of writing new verses due to my shallow social experience. BUT I DON'T GIVE A FUCK. I am just recording my current thoughts, and what is wrong with that?
